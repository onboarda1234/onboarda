Client journey: what the applicant does

These are the steps the person applying on behalf of the company follows in the Onboarda client portal.

Client side
1

Create an account

The applicant enters their name, work email, mobile number, password, and agrees to the portal terms.

✅ Use a work email address.
✅ Save login details safely.
2

Start the company application

The applicant enters the company name, registration number, address, incorporation details, website, and licence information if relevant.

✅ Match names exactly to official documents.
✅ Include full country names and addresses.
3

List the people behind the company

The applicant adds directors, shareholders in the ownership chain, and beneficial owners with 20% or more ownership.

✅ Add date of birth and nationality.
✅ Mark anyone who is a politically exposed person.
4

Explain the business

The applicant describes the sector, countries of operation, target markets, account purpose, expected payments, currencies, source of funds, and management team.

✅ Be specific and consistent.
✅ Explain how money will enter and leave the account.
5

Accept data and monitoring notices

The applicant confirms the information is true and agrees that the business may be checked for compliance and monitored after approval.

✅ Only submit if authorised to represent the company.
6

Review pricing and upload documents

After the first risk check, the applicant accepts the pricing schedule and uploads the required documents.

✅ Upload clear files.
✅ Review the summary before final submission.

What the first risk check means

The system groups applications so the compliance team can spend more time on complex or higher-risk cases. This does not automatically mean approval or rejection.

Decision point
Low risk

Usually moves through standard review with fewer extra questions.

Medium risk

Usually continues to pricing and document submission, with normal officer checks.

High risk

May need an initial officer review before the client can continue.

Very high risk

Requires senior attention and may need extra evidence before any approval.

💡
Simple rule: Higher risk means “look closer,” not “reject automatically.” Officers must still review the facts and evidence.

Document checklist

Documents are grouped so beginners can see what proves the company exists, who controls it, and whether the business activity makes sense.

Checklist

🏢 A — Company documents

Purpose: prove the legal company exists and who is allowed to act for it.

  • Certificate of Incorporation
  • Memorandum or constitution documents
  • Shareholder register
  • Register of directors
  • Latest financial statements or management accounts
  • Proof of registered address
  • Board resolution authorising the signatory
  • Company structure chart
  • Regulatory licence, if applicable
  • Bank reference letter for higher-risk cases, where requested

👤 B — People documents

Purpose: prove the identity and address of directors and key owners.

  • Passport or government identity document for each director
  • Proof of address for each director
  • Passport or government identity document for each beneficial owner
  • Proof of address for each beneficial owner
  • CV or LinkedIn profile where requested
  • Politically exposed person declaration, if relevant
  • Bank reference letter for a politically exposed person, if requested

📄 C — Business evidence

Purpose: help the reviewer understand what the company does. These are usually optional but helpful.

  • Sample customer or supplier contracts
  • Source of wealth evidence
  • Source of funds evidence
  • Recent bank statements
  • AML/CFT policy, if regulated or relevant

📂 D — Other support

Purpose: give extra proof when the normal list does not cover the situation.

  • Legal opinions
  • Compliance certificates
  • Extra contracts or invoices
  • Explanatory letters
  • Any document requested by the compliance officer

Officer journey: how the case is reviewed

The officer uses RegMind to move from “application received” to a clear and recorded decision.

Back office
1

Open the application queue

Filter by status, risk level, assigned officer, or enhanced review status. Open the client’s case.

2

Read the overview

Check company information, people involved, business profile, risk rating, and any previous officer corrections.

3

Review screening results

Look for sanctions, PEP, and any adverse-media signals already included in Sumsub screening data, plus any alerts that need explanation.

4

Check documents

Confirm documents are clear, current, match the application, and support the ownership and business story.

5

Generate and review the memo

The memo summarises the case. The officer validates it, checks any warnings, and signs off that they have reviewed the advisory content.

6

Run supervisor analysis

The supervisor view highlights consistency issues or reasons to escalate. It supports the officer but does not replace human judgment.

7

Record the final action

Approve, decline, request more information, correct a case detail, override with justification, reassign, or escalate.

Possible outcomes and what to do next

Every outcome should be easy for the client and the team to understand.

Final stage

✅ Approve

Use when the information, risk level, documents, screening, memo, and officer review support accepting the client.

  • Record the reason for approval.
  • Confirm any conditions or monitoring requirements.
  • Notify the client that onboarding is complete.

📋 Request more information

Use when the case may be acceptable, but something is missing, unclear, expired, or inconsistent.

  • Ask for specific items only.
  • Use plain language.
  • Review the response before making a final decision.

⬆️ Escalate

Use when a senior officer should review the case, especially for higher-risk, complex, or unusual situations.

  • Explain why escalation is needed.
  • Highlight the key evidence and concerns.
  • Do not approve until escalation is resolved.

❌ Decline

Use when the risks, missing evidence, screening concerns, or business facts do not support onboarding.

  • Record the reason clearly.
  • Keep the audit trail complete.
  • Notify the client using approved wording.

Common mistakes to avoid

Most delays happen because information does not match or documents are incomplete.

Quality tips

For clients

  • Do not use shortened company names if the official document shows the full name.
  • Do not upload blurry or cut-off scans.
  • Do not leave ownership percentages unclear.
  • Do not describe the business differently in different fields.
  • Do not submit before checking the final review page.

For officers

  • Do not rely only on the score; read the case facts.
  • Do not approve while required documents are missing.
  • Do not ignore mismatches between names, ownership, countries, and documents.
  • Do not leave decision notes vague.
  • Do not treat AI advisory text as the final decision.

Simple glossary

Short explanations of words that often appear during onboarding.

No jargon
KYC

“Know Your Customer” — checking who the client is.

AML

Anti-money laundering — checks to reduce financial crime risk.

PEP

A politically exposed person, their close family, or close associates.

UBO

The real person who ultimately owns or controls part of the company.

Risk score

A guide that helps decide how closely the application must be reviewed.

Enhanced review

A closer review for complex or higher-risk applications.

Source of funds

Where the money entering the account will come from.

Source of wealth

How the company or owner built up their overall wealth.

Memo

A written case summary that helps the officer make and record a decision.

Quick use
  1. Client creates an Onboarda account.
  2. Client enters company details, address, licence status, people, business activity, funds, and declarations.
  3. System performs the first risk grouping.
  4. Client accepts pricing if allowed to continue.
  5. Client uploads company documents, identity documents, optional business evidence, and any extra requested files.
  6. Client reviews the application summary and submits.
  7. Officer opens the case in RegMind.
  8. Officer reviews risk, screening, documents, memo, and supervisor findings.
  9. Officer approves, requests more information, escalates, or declines.
  10. If approved, the client is onboarded and moves into ongoing monitoring.
